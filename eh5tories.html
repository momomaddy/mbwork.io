I don't market to Gen Z

There is a shift happening in how brands grow. Gen Z is not just buying products. They are buying into brands that feel like culture, identity, and participation. The brands that win are not the ones with the best features or even the best pricing. They are the ones that create something people want to be part of. This is why some brands feel magnetic while others struggle to stay relevant. It is not about doing more marketing. It is about building something that naturally lives within culture. Here is what that actually looks like. Gen Z does not connect with products alone. They connect with identity, taste, and a clear point of view. The strongest brands build a world that people can recognize instantly and want to step into. LoveShackFancy has done this by creating a fully immersive brand universe rooted in romance, nostalgia, and femininity. This shows up across everything from product design to retail spaces, collaborations, events, and content. The brand feels cohesive no matter where you encounter it. • A consistent aesthetic across every touchpoint • Retail and events that reinforce the brand world • Collaborations that extend the identity into new categories It works because people are not simply buying clothing. They are aligning themselves with a lifestyle and a visual identity that feels distinct. What a brand stands against matters just as much as what it stands for. A clear point of tension makes a brand easier to understand and easier to talk about. Oatly has positioned the dairy industry as its villain and is not subtle about it. From packaging to campaigns, the brand openly challenges traditional dairy in a way that feels bold and intentional. • Messaging that directly calls out the status quo • Packaging that communicates the brand’s stance instantly • A tone that feels confident and slightly provocative It works because conflict creates clarity. When people understand what you are against, it becomes easier for them to decide if they are with you.

On how adapting across client landscapes sharpened my sense of what makes a brand voice distinct...

What I learned the hard way is that you can show up, come up with some pretty smart, clever tone of voice work, hand it over and then realise the company doesn’t know what to do with it. That’s not meant as an insult, but it does raise a point about companies needing to invest in good copywriters so that what you create doesn’t get passed off to teams who don’t know how to implement it. Otherwise you end up thinking, what was it for? A way around that, for me, was always to listen really carefully to the founders or the C-suite (that’s usually who you’re working with). Everyone speaks differently. They might use similar buzzwords, but there’s always nuance. Founders especially are always a bit of a character. You just figure out their unique point of view and explode it into a tone of voice. So I’d write as if I were them, but within the context of the brand we’re building. If you capture them well, there’s at least one person in the business who instinctively understands how it’s supposed to sound, because ultimately the brand voice should sound like them with some added flourishes. When you do that, it feels natural and true because you’re starting with the essence of people in the organisation. This is also why I’ve never had great success on projects where I don’t meet the people. I believe you need to hear how they sound, and then incorporate that into the tone.

Integrating AI into my workflow so that it actually adds strategic value...

The thing I’m most proud of is a chatbot we made for internal use, built off ChatGPT. When I joined, the first job was to create a tone of voice. They had some bullet points at the end of a brand deck — friendly, honest, the usual — and they’d seen brands like Monzo and wanted something in that direction. So I created a proper version for us in the tone I thought we should have. I kept telling people, “This is what we’re supposed to sound like,” hoping they’d absorb it. Easier said than done. Then one day I just pasted the whole thing into ChatGPT and said, “Write like this.” And it did a pretty good job. So I wrote reams of additional material in that tone, including all the postcards we have on the walls at Skyscanner, rewritten as if from fake travellers. I compiled everything into the chatbot along with some rules. The bot now replicates our tone of voice almost flawlessly. It’s not perfect, but it’s very close. I asked what it wanted to be called (it named itself Nico), and I made it available on Slack. It took off immediately. People love it! Someone even used it for their wedding speech, which is wild. It’s given us the consistency we needed. There are so many people who write for the business that we can’t possibly oversee everything. Now things that would’ve felt completely off before at least land in the right tone. It also shows people how much fun you can have with tone. Internally, people often don’t really understand what we do or even know we’re there. But when they use the bot and see its value, they come to us with projects, and it widens our scope of who we work with and what we can do. It’s an upfront investment, but it really pays off once people get interested.

The comments section as primary research

A brand often sees itself in a certain way. But what I’m most curious about is how the audience actually sees it. The space between those two perspectives is where the opportunity lies, so that’s usually where I start. The first thing I always do is start with the audience. I get really curious about who’s using the product or service and what they’re saying online – on social media, Reddit, anywhere people are being brutally honest. Consumers aren’t shy about telling brands what they love, what frustrates them, where they could innovate or what they wish existed. Reading those comments and hearing what people are saying “in the wild” is the best place to begin understanding a brand. I spend a lot of time on Reddit because anonymous users will openly share product feedback or suggestions, point out improvements they want to see or talk about frustrating experiences. It’s a great source of immediate, unfiltered feedback. As a newbie, I also spend a decent amount of time being a sponge. Active listening is key. I absorb as much as I can about how the team works, what they’re building, what they’re brainstorming and what they’re launching. Every industry and brand has its own vernacular, vocabulary and emotional drivers. They’re usually trying to solve a problem, whether that’s changing how they’re perceived externally or innovating through new products. So I’m always looking for where I can slot myself in and add value with my expertise.
